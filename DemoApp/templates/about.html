About Our Project

Understanding the science behind our stroke risk prediction model

Our Mission

The Stroke Risk Prediction project is dedicated to making stroke risk assessment accessible to everyone. Leveraging advanced machine learning algorithms and data from trusted medical research, we’ve developed a tool that helps individuals understand their risk of experiencing a stroke within the next 10 years.

Stroke remains one of the leading causes of disability and death globally. Early identification of risk factors and timely prevention are critical in reducing stroke-related complications. Our tool empowers individuals to evaluate their personal risk and take proactive steps toward protecting their brain and overall vascular health.

The Dataset we choose

Kaggle hosts several stroke-related datasets that are widely used in healthcare research and machine learning projects. One of the most popular is the Stroke Prediction Dataset, which includes medical and demographic information such as age, gender, hypertension, heart disease, glucose levels, BMI, and smoking status.

This dataset is designed to help build predictive models to identify individuals at risk of stroke. Its clean structure and labeled outcomes make it ideal for classification tasks, exploratory data analysis, and developing early-warning systems in digital health applications. Researchers and developers use this data to gain insights into key risk factors and improve clinical decision-making.

Our Methodology

We've implemented multiple machine learning algorithms to analyze the real collected data and create risk prediction models. These include:

  • Support Vector Machine (SVM): A supervised learning algorithm that finds the optimal hyperplane to separate data into different classes with the maximum margin.
  • K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN): A simple, instance-based learning algorithm that classifies data based on the majority label of its ‘k’ nearest neighbors in the feature space.
  • Artificial Neural Network (ANN): A computational model inspired by the human brain, consisting of interconnected nodes (neurons) that learn complex patterns through training.
  • Decision Tree: A tree-structured model used for classification or regression, where decisions are made by splitting data into subsets based on feature values.

By comparing the results from these different algorithms, we can provide a more robust and accurate prediction of heart disease risk.

Risk Factors We Consider

Our prediction model takes into account the following risk factors, all of which have been shown to be significant predictors of stroke:

Factor Description Impact
Sex Male or Female Men generally have a higher risk than pre-menopausal women
Age Age in years Risk increases with age
Smoking status Smoking behavior (never, former, or current smoker) Smoking damages blood vessels and significantly raises stroke risk.
BMI Body Mass Index (kg/m²) Higher BMI is associated with obesity, which increases stroke risk.
Hypertension Whether the person has high blood pressure Hypertension is one of the strongest and most common stroke risk factors.
Heart Disease Whether the person has any cardiovascular disease Heart disease can cause blood clots or irregular rhythms that lead to stroke.
Avg Glucose Level Average blood glucose level (mg/dL) Elevated glucose may indicate diabetes, a condition strongly linked to stroke.

Limitations

While our stroke prediction model is built on validated data and modern machine learning techniques, several limitations should be considered:

  • The dataset used, such as the popular Stroke Prediction Dataset on Kaggle, may not fully represent all ethnicities, age groups, or socioeconomic backgrounds, potentially affecting generalizability.
  • Our tool provides a probabilistic risk estimate—not a medical diagnosis—and should not replace professional evaluation..
  • It is intended to support awareness and early prevention efforts, not serve as a standalone clinical tool..
  • Additionally, some individual risk factors—such as family history, rare medical conditions, or genetic markers—may not be fully accounted for in the current model.
